As the tech continues to evolve, software becomes an increaingly central part of our lives. From the apps on our phones to the software running our cars, it's everywhere. But have you ever stopped to think about the environmental impact of all this software? Everytime a  piece of code runs, it triggers energy usage inside the CPU, GPU, and other hardware components \cite{greenuk_footprint}. This subsequent usage of software adds up to the carbon emission a device generates \cite{thoughtworks_carbon_intensity}. The carbon footprint of software is a growing concern, as it contributes to climate change and has a significant impact on our planet. 

The scale of this concern is larger than most people realize. The information and communication technology (ICT) sector accounts for an estimated of 2.1\% to 3.9\% of the global greenhouse gas emissions \cite{ict_stat}. The global data center energy consumption alone is projected to reach around 945TWh by 2030, which is a significant increase from 153TWh in 2005, 205TWh in 2018. \cite{iea_energy_consumption} \cite{masanet_energy_consumption}. This figures suggest that the energy consumed by software system is no longer a concern confined to hardware engineering, but it falls within the scope of software development descisions. 

One of the most consequential and least-studied of those decisions is the choice of programming language.
